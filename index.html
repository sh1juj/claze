The Problem

Cement is a significant and structural source of CO₂

Cement production accounts for roughly 7% of global CO₂ emissions. Most of those emissions are process-related — they come from making clinker, not from burning fuel — which means switching to zero-carbon energy alone does not resolve the problem.

Rising Demand

Cement demand is projected to grow, particularly across the global south, where large-scale construction activity is expected to increase.

₤

Cost Sensitivity

Cement is a commodity with thin margins. Solutions requiring significant cost premiums face structural adoption barriers.

CO₂

Process Emissions

Around 60% of cement emissions come from calcining limestone to make clinker. Reducing clinker content is the primary decarbonisation lever.

Demand for low-carbon cement is growing. The constraint is supply of affordable, scalable alternatives.
The Solution

Calcined clay from waste heat,
without additional fuel.

Claze is developing a process that uses high-temperature waste heat — from steelmaking and other industrial operations — to calcine clay. The resulting material can be used as a supplementary cementitious material (SCM), reducing the clinker content and carbon intensity of cement.

Energy

No Additional Fuel

The process is designed to run on waste heat that industrial sites already generate. No separate fuel source is needed for calcination.

Economics

Low Capital Requirements

The approach is designed to integrate with existing infrastructure rather than require dedicated new kilns or greenfield construction.

Resource

Abundant Feedstock

Clay is widely available, including in regions where cement demand is highest, offering potential for geographically distributed deployment.

Operations

Designed to Be Non-Disruptive

The process is intended to operate alongside existing industrial activity without interrupting core plant operations.

Why Now

Converging pressures are creating a clear market gap

Regulatory pressure, ESG commitments, and the structural decline of traditional SCM sources are creating demand for new decarbonisation pathways in the cement sector — at a time when the industry is looking for practical, cost-effective solutions.

01

Traditional SCM supply is declining

Fly ash and GGBS — the most widely used SCMs — are becoming less available as coal power and blast-furnace steelmaking reduce output.

02

Construction demand is growing

A substantial portion of the built environment projected for 2050 has yet to be constructed, concentrated in regions with limited low-carbon material supply.

03

Regulatory and industry pressure

Carbon pricing schemes, green procurement standards, and corporate net-zero commitments are increasing the urgency for verifiable emission reductions in construction materials.

Process

From waste heat to low-carbon cement

The core concept is straightforward: use heat that industrial operations already produce, apply it to calcine clay, and supply the resulting material as an SCM to cement producers.

Benefits

Designed to be capital-light, low-carbon, and compatible with existing operations.

For Industrial Partners
  • Potential to reduce reported emissions

    Economic allocation of waste heat recovery may support improvements in reported scope 1 or scope 2 figures.

  • Revenue from waste streams

    Waste heat that is currently dissipated could generate value through a commercial arrangement with Claze.

  • No change to core operations

    The process is designed to be additive — integration should not require modifications to existing plant workflows.

For Cement Producers
  • Access to a scalable SCM source

    Calcined clay is not dependent on fly ash or GGBS supply, which are both structurally declining.

  • Lower clinker-to-cement ratio

    A quality SCM enables cement producers to reduce clinker content and the associated process emissions.

  • Drop-in compatibility

    Calcined clay is an established SCM with existing standards frameworks. It does not require reformulation of cement products.
